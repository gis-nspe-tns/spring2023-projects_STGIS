Morgan Gamblin | Using GIS to Find a Home

The world is filled with uncertainty, making planning without deep research feel overwhelming. With this project I intend to search for the 'best' place to live. Through a combination of qualitative wants and needs, spatial evaluation within a suitability context was conducted.

This project aims to answer the following three questions:

What counties are in close proximity to a big city?
Where is access to nature readily available?
What areas are low risk for natural disasters?

US Census data, national risk data, and Copernicus Global Land Cover information for the United States were used as primary data sources to ascertain suitable sites meeting the project's 'best' criteria.

Vivienne Taylor | Sea Level Rise Along the California Coast

Sea level rise is a threat that is rapidly becoming more and more of a concern for global populations. As the climate crisis continues, sea level rise is an issue many countries are now facing. Within the United States, coastal states need to pay particular attention to the issue of sea level rise and how it may affect populations and land use. Through this project potential effects of rising sea levels along the California coast is explored from a spatial perspective. This project specifically examines the intersection of land use and population and how the two would be impacted by various levels of sea level rise. The multiple maps produced show that on average in each scenario the types of land that are most at risk of inundation- urban land, wetland, and cropland. Analysis results also show that the majority of coastal populations reside in decidedly urban areas.

Trey Thiessen | Mapping The Phenomenon of Gentrification in NYC: Housing, Income, and Infrastructure

Gentrification is a multifaceted process and phenomenon that within the past 40+ years has altered the social, economic, and physical cultures/identities across U.S. cities and neighborhoods. New York City communities, especially those of low-income and/or POC residents, have experienced various forms and levels of gentrification usually attributed to growing rates of white residents moving into the area or wealthy developers, investors, or speculators building/fixing new higher-cost of living infrastructure. Through this spatial analysis of New York City, a ten-year span of white income and white household percentages, along with construction projects (demolition, alteration, and new infrastructure) are assessed at NYC census tracts. Results suggest that gentrification has occurred throughout Manhattan and Brooklyn, while Queens, the Bronx and Staten Island feature discrete hotspots of both very gentrified and very high levels of construction.
